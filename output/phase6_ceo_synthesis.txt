  2. DRIVER DEEP-DIVE #1: PARTNER/ROHIT QUALITY
     "The Partner IS the Brand"
============================================================================

  EVIDENCE STRENGTH: HIGH (all 3 perspectives converge)

  QUANTITATIVE
  - partner_avg_nps is #1 KDA consensus driver (4/4 statistical methods)
  - 100% sprint stability (appears in every sprint tested)
  - partner_churn_rate is #2 churn predictor (11.4% feature importance)
  - partner_sla_compliance, partner_fcr_rate in extended top-20
  - Partner quality explains more NPS variance than any network metric

  QUALITATIVE
  - technician_partner_bad theme: 70.6% Detractors, 23.5% churn rate
  - complaint_resolution_bad: 85.1% Detractors, NPS 2.4
  - Customer voices:
    "Technician nahi aate" (technician doesn't come)
    "Rohit ne bola 100 Mbps milega, magar 5-6 hi aati hai"
    (Rohit promised 100 Mbps, but only getting 5-6)
    "Ek hafte se complaint hai, koi dhyan nahi deta"
    (Complaint for a week, nobody pays attention)

  INDUSTRY EXPERT VIEW
  - Independent finding #3: "The Partner IS the Brand"
  - Partner quality variance > geography variance
  - "A bad partner = bad Wiom, regardless of network quality"
  - This is a known pattern in last-mile ISPs: the field tech shapes
    the ENTIRE perception of the service

  OPERATIONAL LEVER
  Snowflake: partner_details_log (partner status), PARTNER_INFLUX_SUMMARY
  (partner uptime), service_ticket_model (partner SLA), t_router_user_mapping
  (partner customer portfolio)

  RECOMMENDED ACTION
  1. Build Partner Quality Composite Score:
     - Partner uptime % (30-day avg)
     - Median install TAT
     - Ticket SLA compliance rate
     - Customer churn rate in portfolio
     - Average NPS of their customers
  2. Tier partners A/B/C based on composite score
  3. A-tier: higher commissions, priority install allocation
  4. C-tier: mandatory training, 90-day improvement window, supervised
  5. Auto-escalate any complaint from C-tier partner customers

  EXPECTED IMPACT: Moving bottom-quartile partners to median could shift
  15-20% of their Detractors toward Passive/Promoter. Estimated NPS
  improvement: +3 to +5 points within 2 sprints of implementation.

  2. Repeat complaint alert: 2nd ticket in 7 days = auto-escalate
  3. Target: reduce AVG_TIMES_REOPENED from 0.14 to <0.05
  4. Fast-track first-30-day complaints (critical retention window)
  5. Track FCR at customer level (not just system-level SLA)

  EXPECTED IMPACT: Reducing repeat complaints by 30% could convert
  200-300 Detractors to Passives, improving NPS by +3-5 points.

  4. DRIVER DEEP-DIVE #3: NETWORK STABILITY
     "Customers talk about it most, but we can't measure it well"
============================================================================

  EVIDENCE STRENGTH: MEDIUM-HIGH (qual + industry agree; quant limited)

  THIS IS A MEASUREMENT GAP, NOT DRIVER IRRELEVANCE

  What customers say (STRONG signal):
  - slow_speed: 877 comments (16.0% of classified) — #1 theme by volume
  - disconnection_frequency: 676 comments (12.3%) — #3 theme
  - internet_down_outage: 147 comments (2.7%)
  - Combined network complaints: 1,700+ comments = 31% of all themes
  - "100 Mbps bola tha, aaati 5-6 hi hai" (Promised 100, getting 5-6)

  What our models see (WEAK signal):
  - avg_uptime_pct: NOT in NPS KDA consensus top-8
  - Only 0.9% gap in PEAK_UPTIME_PCT between Detractors and Promoters
  - BUT: avg_uptime_pct IS #3 churn predictor (11.1% importance)
  - AND: For 6+ month customers, uptime becomes #1 churn predictor

  WHY THE GAP:
  - Uptime measured at PARTNER FLEET level, not customer device level
  - A partner with 71% uptime may have customers at 95% and 50%
  - Speed data is COMPLETELY MISSING from Snowflake at customer level
  - The #1 customer complaint has NO statistical representation

  INDUSTRY EXPERT VIEW
  - "Connection stability > speed for this segment"
  - First outage experience is Breakage Point #1
  - "95% monthly uptime can hide 6 hours of prime-time downtime"

  RECOMMENDED ACTION
  1. Deploy device-level speed testing (periodic automated tests)
  2. Customer-experienced uptime tracking (device ping monitoring)
  3. Peak-hour QoE monitoring (6-11 PM family usage window)
  4. WiFi signal strength mapping at installation

  EXPECTED IMPACT: Closing this measurement gap would enable targeted
  interventions for individual customers instead of partner-level averages.
  Cannot quantify NPS impact until device-level data is available.

  5. DRIVER DEEP-DIVE #4: 28-DAY BILLING CYCLE
     "A self-inflicted competitive wound"
============================================================================

  EVIDENCE STRENGTH: MEDIUM-HIGH (qual + industry strong; quant indirect)

  THE MATH CUSTOMERS DO
  - 28-day cycle = 13 billing cycles per year
  - Competitors (JioFiber, Airtel) use 30-day = 12 cycles per year
  - Extra cycle cost: Rs 500-600/year at typical plan prices
  - For a Rs 399/month customer, that's 8.3% more expensive annually

  QUALITATIVE EVIDENCE
  - billing_28day theme: 57 comments, 73.7% Detractors, NPS 3.0
  - Churn rate: 31.6% (4th highest of any theme)
  - Even PROMOTERS complain about it — Kano Reverse Quality
  - The irritation COMPOUNDS with tenure: 69.8% from 6+ month bucket
  - Customer voice: "Ye to upbhoktaon ke saath seedha dhokha hai"
    (This is outright fraud against consumers)

  INDUSTRY EXPERT VIEW
  - Finding #1: "Strategic Vulnerability"
  - "Recurring reminder that Wiom charges more than competitors"
  - Every recharge is a moment where the customer does competitive math
  - This is the EASIEST competitor talking point: "We charge monthly,
    they charge every 28 days"

  WHY IT MATTERS BEYOND THE MONEY
  - It signals UNFAIRNESS — customers feel cheated, not just overcharged
  - It creates a negative touchpoint 13x per year
  - It gives competitors an easy differentiation message
  - It disproportionately hurts loyal customers who stay long enough
    to notice the pattern

  RECOMMENDED ACTION
  Option A: Move to 30-day billing cycle (clean, permanent fix)
  Option B: Add 2 "bonus days" per cycle for 6+ month customers
  Revenue impact: ~3.7% fewer recharge events/year (13 -> 12.2)
  Offset: reduced churn + eliminated competitive disadvantage

  EXPECTED IMPACT: Eliminates a Reverse Quality entirely. Could prevent
  18-30 churns per sprint among billing-sensitive customers.

  6. DRIVER DEEP-DIVE #5: PRICE/VALUE PERCEPTION
     "Affordability is Wiom's #1 moat — protect it"
============================================================================

  EVIDENCE STRENGTH: MEDIUM-HIGH (qual + industry strong; quant moderate)

  THE GOOD NEWS: AFFORDABILITY IS THE #1 PROMOTER DRIVER
  - pricing_affordable theme: NPS 8.6, churn 6.4% (LOWEST of any theme)
  - "Sasta hai, koi extra charge nahi" (It's cheap, no hidden charges)
  - "Paise vasool" (Value for money)
  - Wiom wins on price-to-quality ratio vs JioFiber (Rs 399 vs Rs 399+)

  THE BAD NEWS: PRICE SENSITIVITY IS A DOUBLE-EDGED SWORD
  - pricing_expensive theme: NPS 5.3, churn 28.9%
  - Customers who feel overcharged churn at 4.5x the rate of those
    who feel Wiom is affordable (28.9% vs 6.4%)
  - The 28-day billing cycle erodes the affordability perception

  INDUSTRY EXPERT VIEW
  - Ranked #3 in ISP driver taxonomy (value perception)
  - "Price-to-quality ratio vs alternatives is the key metric"
  - Low switching costs amplify price sensitivity in this segment
  - JTBD: Wiom wins on "affordable internet for children's education"

  RECOMMENDED ACTION
  1. DO NOT increase prices without proportional value addition
  2. Address 28-day perception (see Driver #4)
  3. Transparent pricing communication at onboarding
  4. Communicate total value (speed + reliability + support) not just price
  5. Monitor competitor pricing in each geography monthly

  EXPECTED IMPACT: Protecting the affordability moat maintains Wiom's
  primary competitive advantage. Price increases without visible value
  improvements would trigger outsized churn in this price-sensitive base.

  The Industry Expert worked independently of both the CX and Stats teams.
  Their assessment provides an external benchmark for Wiom's findings.

  WHERE WIOM'S FINDINGS ALIGN WITH ISP BEST PRACTICE
  1. Partner quality as top driver: Consistent with last-mile ISP pattern
     where field technicians shape the entire service perception
  2. Support effort > problem frequency: Classic finding in telecom CX
  3. Affordability as #1 moat: Correct positioning for lower-middle-income
  4. 28-day billing vulnerability: Known competitive risk in Indian ISP market
  5. Bimodal NPS distribution: Common in emerging-market ISPs with binary
     service experience (works perfectly or doesn't work at all)

  BLIND SPOTS: DRIVERS THAT SHOULD MATTER BUT WEREN'T SURFACED
  These are areas where ISP industry knowledge suggests important drivers
  exist, but our data couldn't capture them:

  1. Peak-hour quality of experience (6-11 PM family usage)
     - No hourly speed/uptime data at customer level
     - "Slow" complaints may be peak-hour contention, not always-on issue

  2. DNS resolution speed
     - "Pages load slow" but bandwidth may be fine
     - Not measured in any Snowflake table

  3. WiFi interference & RF environment
     - Neighbor routers, walls, microwaves affect indoor WiFi quality
     - Customer blames Wiom but issue is environmental

  4. Device limitations
     - Old smartphones can't utilize available bandwidth
     - Customer perceives "slow internet" when device is the bottleneck

  5. Power supply instability
     - Unstable power causes router reboots = intermittent connectivity
     - Common in tier-2/3 areas (UP, parts of Delhi NCR)

  6. Competitive context by geography
     - NPS interpretation needs local competitive benchmark
     - NPS 5 where JioFiber operates is worse than NPS 5 without alternatives

============================================================================
  10. APPENDIX: DATA QUALITY & METHODOLOGY NOTES
============================================================================

  A. WHAT THE DATA CAN TELL US (High Confidence)
  - Which PARTNERS drive the most dissatisfaction (and churn)
  - Whether SUPPORT HANDLING predicts NPS and churn
  - Which TENURE STAGE is most vulnerable
  - Whether 28-DAY BILLING correlates with churn
  - Whether NPS SCORE adds value beyond operational data for churn
  - Overall RISK SCORES for proactive intervention

  B. WHAT THE DATA CANNOT TELL US (Known Limitations)
  1. Speed data is missing: The #1 customer complaint has no metric
  2. Uptime is too coarse: Partner-level masks individual experience
  3. Outage data covers only ~20 days (Dec 20 - Jan 8, 2026)
  4. Only 42% of respondents wrote comments (selection bias)
  5. Churn model AUC of 0.977 is high partly due to total_payments
     feature (more payments = longer active = not churned)
  6. Install TAT analysis has insufficient sample for new customers
     (only 25 in 0-15 day bucket)
  7. Competitive context is not in the data (no neighborhood-level)
  8. Promoter churn (20.2%) is abnormally high and partially unexplained

  C. METHODOLOGY SUMMARY
  - Phase 0: 13,045 NPS responses parsed from Excel, joined to Snowflake
  - Phase 1: 5,488 Hindi/Hinglish/English comments processed through
    NLP pipeline (cleaning, transliteration, translation, sentiment)
    23 emergent themes discovered via unsupervised clustering
  - Phase 2: CX frameworks applied (SERVQUAL, Kano, CES, JTBD)
  - Phase 2b: Independent ISP industry expert assessment
  - Phase 3: 200+ features engineered from Snowflake operational data
  - Phase 4: Key Driver Analysis (4 methods: Ridge, Random Forest,
    Gradient Boosting, Partial Correlation), churn models (AUC 0.977),
    customer segmentation (6 clusters), temporal stability analysis
  - Phase 5: Triangulation of qualitative, quantitative, and industry
    expert findings; confidence-rated unified driver ranking
  - Phase 6: This leadership synthesis

  D. DATA LEAKAGE CONTROLS
  Excluded from churn model to prevent artificial accuracy:
  - days_since_last_recharge (directly encodes churn definition)
  - sprint_num (right-censoring proxy)
  - recharge_regularity, total_recharges (activity proxies)
  - Sprints 12-13 excluded (insufficient churn observation time)
